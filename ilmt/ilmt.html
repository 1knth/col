I Lost My Talk

BIG IDEAS

  • Understanding
  • Forgiveness
  • Resilience

QUOTES

“I speak like you, I think like you, I create like you”

‎

“So gently, I offer my hand and ask, let me find my talk so I can teach you about me.”

In the modern world, we often see a struggle in finding one’s voice. Self-expression has become uncommon because our society constantly tries to diminish individual identities. This problem is addressed in “I Lost My Talk” by Rita Joe when she says “I speak like you, I think like you, I create like you” (Joe 7-8). Throughout the poem, she urges us to embrace our unique stories and reclaim our power to be heard. Joe sheds light on the loss of identity and cultural heritage endured in her community. The poem acts as a joint-effort cry for reclamation in the face of today's society. Her poem not only inspires Indigenous communities to speak out for themselves but has also reminded me of my knowledge regarding the painful consequences of colonization and the removal of culture like the ongoing xenophobia happening in America where my family resides. Joe challenges the notion of a singular narrative or perspective and encourages a more inclusive understanding of the diverse history and experiences faced by Indigenous people. I often thought for many years of my life that showing kindness to people that have wronged you makes you weak but, despite the ignorance of the Western world, Joe is patient, she “gently” advocates and “offers her hand” to educate those who are willing to listen (Joe 13-15). She sets an example for her community and promotes a passive solution to this generational conflict by symbolizing that you can’t fight fire with fire. She taught me that being the bigger person in any situation would never have any consequences even if things don’t go as planned.
